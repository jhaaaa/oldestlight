The oldest light in the universe

The universe started with the Big Bang — 13.8 billion years ago. For the first 380,000 years it was so hot that light couldn't travel through it. Everything was opaque.

Then, 380,000 years in, the universe cooled just enough for electrons and protons to combine into hydrogen atoms. The moment that happened, the universe became transparent — and all the light that had been trapped was released at once.

That light is still traveling. It has been crossing space for 13.8 billion years, in every direction. It fills all of space equally. It is 2.7 degrees above absolute zero. When you point a detector at any point in the sky, you detect it.

This is the Cosmic Microwave Background. Not an echo of the Big Bang. The actual light from that moment, still arriving.

The map

The map on this site is the Planck 2018 SMICA full-sky temperature map, released by the European Space Agency. It was assembled from five years of observations by the Planck satellite. It contains 50 million pixels, each one a temperature measurement accurate to a few millionths of a degree.

The tiny variations in color — the faint red and blue patches — are temperature fluctuations of roughly ±300 millionths of a degree around the average of 2.7K. These are the imprints of quantum events in the first fraction of a second of existence. The seeds of every galaxy, every star, every planet. Including this one.

The codec

Each pixel in the map has a temperature. That temperature is a number. Numbers can be converted to letters. Letters can be searched for words.

The conversion is simple and deterministic — the same coordinates always produce the same letters, always produce the same words. No interpretation. No editorial choice. The codec is a lens, not an author. 

temperature  →  shift to positive  →  scale to 0–255  →  remainder of ÷26  →  letter A–Z

A patch of sky becomes a stream of hundreds of letters. A word lattice then finds the longest real English words that tile that stream, reading left to right — words that emerged from physics, not chosen, but found.

The transmission

When you click a patch of sky for the first time, the signal words from that location are passed to an AI that speaks as the CMB itself — not a metaphor for it, but the thing. 13.8 billion years old. Present everywhere at once. Non-human in syntax and reference.

The signal words are used as seeds. A word like LIF may surface as life or lifting or half-life. HAP may become happen or perhaps. The root matters more than the exact form.

The transmission is generated once per location and cached. Every person who visits that patch of sky afterward reads what the first visitor received. The CMB doesn't change. Neither does what it said.

The Cold Spot

The CMB Cold Spot — at galactic coordinates l=264°, b=48° — is the largest anomaly in the Cosmic Microwave Background. It is colder than its surroundings by about 70 millionths of a degree. No agreed-upon explanation exists.
